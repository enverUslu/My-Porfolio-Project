Introduction¶

In the present study, an examination will be conducted on economic datasets obtained from the Gapminder website (https://www.gapminder.org/data/) on September 27th, 2020. The aim is to explore the trends resulting from the increasing prosperity observed in various countries. Multiple variables will be utilized to measure these trends, namely:

  • Individual electricity consumption
  • Per capita income (PPP)
  • Gross Domestic Product (GDP) measured in terms of PPP

About PPP
The abbreviation PPP stands for Purchasing Power Parity, which serves as an economic indicator for comparing the prices of goods and services across different nations. It offers a metric to evaluate economic productivity and living standards disparities between countries (https://www.investopedia.com/updates/purchasing-power-parity-ppp/).

Throughout our data analysis, we aim to address the following inquiries:

  • Have specific countries displayed superior growth in the selected metrics compared to others?
  • How does the growth of income differ between China and India, the two prominent developing economies?
  • Is there a correlation between increasing levels of per capita income and electricity consumption per individual?

Please Note:
Although six variables were initially considered for analysis, insufficient data compelled us to focus solely on the three variables mentioned above. The Data Wrangling section encompasses the handling of the additional variables as well; however, for the purpose of this study, they have been excluded.

Data Cleaning¶

Upon closer inspection, it becomes evident that there is a significant disparity in the amount of missing data across different dimensions. To address this issue, we will employ interpolation for the years with missing data within the available range, and extrapolation for the years outside the observed range. These operations will be performed on a per-country basis.

To carry out this data cleaning process, we will refer to the following resource for guidance: https://towardsdatascience.com/using-pandas-transform-and-apply-to-deal-with-missing-data-on-a-group-level-cb6ccf060531.

Overall, aside from the missing data, the rest of the dataset appears to be in good condition.

# Fill in empty cells
# Define helper function
def fill_missing(grp):
    res = grp.set_index('year')\
    .interpolate(method='linear',limit=5)\
    .fillna(method='ffill')\
    .fillna(method='bfill')
    del res['country']
    return res
    
# Group by country name and fill missing
df = df_merged.groupby(['country']).apply(
    lambda grp: fill_missing(grp)
)
df = df.reset_index()

# Drop rows with empty data and save to a new dataframe
df = df.dropna()

Research Question 1: Have certain countries exhibited better growth in the selected metrics compared to others?¶

To investigate this research question, we will focus on analyzing the economic growth of China, India, and Japan in comparison to the United States. The time period under consideration spans from 1965 to 2013, taking into account the availability of data.

Among these Asian countries, we anticipate the following trends based on historical observations:

  • Japan: Expectations suggest rapid economic growth during the 1970s and 1980s, positioning Japan as one of the world's fastest-growing economies. The period known as "The Lost Decade" (1991-2001) is characterized by economic stagnation in Japan, primarily caused by the Japanese Asset Bubble.
  • China: Starting from 1978, China's economic reforms initiated by Deng Xiaoping, the then Chairman of the Chinese Communist Party, are expected to have led to significant economic growth.
  • India: Economic growth in India is anticipated to have surged from the 1990s onwards, triggered by the policies implemented by Prime Minister Narasimha Rao and Finance Minister Manmohan Singh.

By analyzing the available data, we aim to gain insights into the growth trajectories of these countries and compare their economic performances during the specified time frame.

Research Question 1 Analysis¶

This graph highlights in detail the economic performance between these four countries over the past few decades since 1965.

Japan:¶

In 1965, Japan's economy was approximately 70.2% the size of the United States. However, it experienced a significant drop in 1982 due to the Asset Price Bubble, leading to "The Lost Decade" from 1991 to 2001. It took nearly 20 years for Japan's economy to recover to its 1981 level, reaching around $4 trillion in the year 2000.

China and India:¶

From 1965 to 1980, both China and India exhibited similar economic performance. China's economic reforms, initiated in 1978, resulted in a notable increase in GDP growth. By 1985, China's GDP surpassed India's and was slightly more than twice its size at $264 billion.

China overtook Japan as the world's second-largest economy (measured in PPP) in 1993. Joining the World Trade Organization in 2001 further accelerated China's growth, as evidenced by the steeper positive gradient in the graph.

In contrast, India's growth has been more moderate and has not experienced the rapid bursts seen in China. It took India 11 years, from 2002 to 2013, to catch up to China's economy of $6.63 trillion.

United States:¶

The United States remained the world's largest economy from 1965 to 2013. The World Trade Center terrorist attacks in 2001 did not have a significant impact on the US economy. However, the Global Financial Crisis, occurring from 2007 to 2009, resulted in an overall contraction of 3.87 percent in US GDP. The economy dropped from USD15.5 trillion to USD14.9 trillion during this period, and it took until 2011 for the US to regain its pre-recession levels.

These observations provide a detailed analysis of the economic performance of Japan, China, India, and the United States over the past few decades, highlighting key milestones and challenges in each country's growth trajectory.
